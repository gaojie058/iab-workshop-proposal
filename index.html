Interpreting Agent Behavior

Empirical Methods for Understanding Agents
Overview

Motivation

As commercial agent systems such as Claude Code and Devin see widespread real-world deployment in early 2026, their behaviors have grown increasingly complex. These systems autonomously plan, execute, and iterate on multi-step tasks, generating vast amounts of behavioral data. Yet current evaluation relies almost exclusively on automated benchmarks: pass/fail metrics that reveal whether an agent succeeded but not how it behaved, why it failed, or how humans made sense of its actions. Closing this gap is essential for building agents that are not only effective but also interpretable, debuggable, and trustworthy.

The Interpreting Agent Behavior (IAB) workshop investigates agent behavior from three complementary perspectives: (1) How do agents behave?, including how they make decisions, propose and revise plans, select and use tools, fail, and recover from errors; (2) How do humans respond?, including how users write and adapt prompts, verify agent-generated outputs, and monitor ongoing executions; and (3) How do they interact?, including how humans and agents communicate intent, adjust plans mid-execution, and handle unexpected failures. We aim to develop shared methods, resources, and vocabulary for interpreting agent behavior, with a particular focus on empirical approaches drawn from the social sciences and human-computer interaction.

Scope

Proposed Research Questions

We plan to investigate agent behavior from three complementary perspectives.

Agent Behavior

  • Decision-making under uncertainty
  • Failure cascades and error recovery
  • Multi-agent coordination and emergent behaviors
  • Behavioral differences across LLM backbones

Human Response

  • Trust calibration and over-reliance
  • Verification strategies for agent errors
  • Mental model formation and updating
  • Cognitive sense-making processes

Human–Agent Interaction

  • Systematic coding of interaction traces
  • Co-evolution of instructions and responses
  • Interface effects on behavior and trust
  • Communication breakdowns and task failures

Submissions

Planned Call for Papers

If accepted, we will welcome submissions on new results, negative findings, benchmarks, position papers, and system demos. All submissions would be non-archival.

We particularly encourage work that addresses how to study agent behavior, including but not limited to:

  • Behavioral annotation frameworks and taxonomies
  • Interaction trace analysis and visualization
  • Human-subject experimental protocols for studying agent use
  • Mixed-methods approaches bridging ML evaluation with HCI or social science methodologies
  • Benchmarks and metrics for process-level (not just outcome-level) evaluation

Long Papers

Up to 9 pages + references. Empirical studies, benchmarks, or comprehensive analyses.

Short Papers

Up to 4 pages + references. Position papers, demos, preliminary findings, or negative results.

Review Process

Conference style. Single-anonymous. At least 2 reviews per submission via OpenReview.
